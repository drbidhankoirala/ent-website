Thyroid Cancer Management

Thyroid cancer is highly treatable when managed correctly. The key is accurate surgery with preservation of vital structures.

Early Signs

  • Neck swelling / thyroid nodule
  • Voice change (hoarseness)
  • Difficulty swallowing
  • Neck lymph node enlargement

Risk Factors

  • Iodine imbalance
  • Radiation exposure (childhood)
  • Family history
  • Female gender
  • Long-standing goiter

Final Staging

  • TNM Classification (AJCC 8th edition)
  • Final staging after surgery (Histopathology – pTNM)

Types & Prognosis

Papillary (PTC) – OS >95%, DFS 85–90%

Follicular (FTC) – OS 80–90%, DFS 70–85%

Medullary (MTC) – OS 60–80%

Anaplastic (ATC) – OS <10–20%

Treatment

  • FNAC (Bethesda)
  • Surgery (Hemi/Total thyroidectomy)
  • Neck dissection
  • Radioactive iodine (RAI)
  • TSH suppression

Post-Thyroidectomy Hypocalcemia

  • Tingling, cramps due to low calcium
  • Usually temporary
  • Rarely permanent

Expert surgery reduces this risk significantly.

Voice Change After Surgery

  • Temporary: 3–10%
  • Permanent: <1%
  • Symptoms: hoarseness, weak voice

Proper nerve preservation minimizes risk.

Why High-Volume Oncosurgeon?

  • Lower complication rates
  • Better nerve preservation
  • Better long-term outcomes

Why Head & Neck Oncosurgeon?

  • Precise RLN & EBSLN preservation
  • Parathyroid preservation
  • Complete cancer removal
  • Expert neck dissection
