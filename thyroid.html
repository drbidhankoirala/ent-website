Understanding Thyroid Cancer

Thyroid cancer arises from thyroid cells and shows a wide spectrum — from slow-growing tumors to aggressive disease. Most cases are detected early due to imaging.

Many thyroid cancers have no symptoms initially and are found incidentally.

Types of Thyroid Cancer

Papillary Follicular Medullary Anaplastic
Papillary (Most common)
Excellent prognosis, slow growing.
Follicular
Can spread via blood (bone/lung).
Medullary
Arises from C-cells, may be genetic.
Anaplastic
Rare, aggressive, requires urgent care.

Symptoms

Neck lump (moves on swallowing)
Voice change
Difficulty swallowing
Breathing difficulty
No symptoms in early stages in many patients.

Diagnosis

Ultrasound (first test)
FNAC (key test)
Blood tests (TSH)
CT/MRI (advanced cases)

FNAC & Bethesda System

Bethesda I–II: Benign
Bethesda III–IV: Indeterminate
Bethesda V: Suspicious
Bethesda VI: Malignant
Limitation: FNAC cannot always confirm cancer — final diagnosis often requires surgery.

Prognosis & Survival

5-year survival ≈ 98% in most patients.

Large population data shows excellent outcomes, especially for early-stage disease. Survival depends on type, age, and spread.

Early stage: Very high cure (>90–95%)
Locally advanced: Good with treatment
Metastatic: Variable, needs advanced care

Final staging is confirmed after surgery.

What Happens if Untreated?

If not treated, thyroid cancer can gradually increase in size and invade nearby structures.

Airway compression
Difficulty swallowing
Voice nerve involvement
Spread to lung or bone
Some tumors are slow, but delay can make treatment more complex.

Treatment

Surgery (main treatment)
Radioiodine (oral therapy)
Radiation (selected cases)
Targeted therapy (advanced)
